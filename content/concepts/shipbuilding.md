## Summary
This theme argues that naval and maritime industrial capacity is becoming strategically important again as defense priorities broaden beyond software and missiles. The uploaded PDF’s basket graphic makes clear that the setup favors not just one or two obvious primes but a wider stack of yards, propulsion providers, electronics names, and maritime suppliers if policy support keeps building.

## Notable expressions
- [[general-dynamics]], [[huntington-ingalls]], and [[thyssenkrupp-marine-systems]] are the clearest large-cap or pure-play anchors.
- The broader basket adds [[fincantieri]], [[mitsubishi-heavy-industries]], [[hd-hyundai-heavy-industries]], [[hanwha-ocean]], [[samsung-heavy-industries]], and [[austal]] as yard-capacity expressions.
- The supplier layer includes [[bwx-technologies]], [[rolls-royce]], [[wartsila]], [[kongsberg-gruppen]], [[hensoldt]], [[furuno]], [[chugoku-marine-paints]], [[graham-corporation]], [[esco-technologies]], and [[conrad-shipyards]].

## Uploaded PDF visual pass
- The page-63 Naval Shipbuilding Basket confirms this is a broad maritime industrial-capacity thesis rather than a single-country naval-prime trade.
- The basket spans military and commercial shipbuilders alongside power systems, sensors, coatings, and marine-electronics suppliers.
- That breadth makes [[shipbuilding]] overlap more naturally with [[geopolitical-special-situations]] and with the hidden-bottleneck logic seen elsewhere in [[26-trades-for-2026]].
